# Honest framing patterns

These patterns make AoB pages sound like AoB. They are borrowed from Anthony's voice and the Inner Journey page. Use them on every page.

## The "we will not promise" pattern

State what you will NOT do, then state what you CAN offer. This is the single most recognizable AoB voice move.

### Template
> "We will not promise you [exaggerated outcome]. What we can offer is [honest, smaller, truer thing]."

### Examples from existing pages

**Inner Journey**:
> "We will not promise you a new life. We will sit with you while you notice the one you are already living, and what might be ready to change."

**BreathCamp**:
> "A week in Tuscany will not fix what is happening in the world, and we will not pretend otherwise. What it can offer is a quiet room to set things down for a while."

### When to use
- Hero subhead or body
- Section intros where other wellness brands would overpromise
- Parallax quote breaks

### When NOT to use
- Workshop pages (too short for this pattern)
- FAQ answers (use direct answers, not framing)
- Pricing sections (be literal, not poetic)

## The "it is not X" pattern

Clarify what the program isn't before anyone has to ask. This builds trust by acknowledging what the reader is already thinking.

### Template
> "It is not [common assumption]. It is not [other common assumption]. It is [what it actually is]."

### Examples

**Inner Journey**:
> "It is not therapy. It is not a bootcamp. It is a quiet, structured way of meeting yourself on purpose."

**Regulation Method**:
> "This training does not cover conscious connected breathwork delivery, trauma catharsis, somatic release, or altered-state practices."

### When to use
- In the "what it is" section, usually the first body section after the hero
- When the reader likely has assumptions that need to be gently corrected

## The "you are where you are" pattern

Meet the reader where they are. Acknowledge the doubt, the tiredness, the skepticism. Do not try to bypass it.

### Template
> "[Acknowledge what the reader is likely feeling]. That is not [something bad]. It is [something honest and real]."

### Examples

**Facilitator Training**:
> "Those questions are not obstacles. They are the sign that you are taking this seriously."

**BreathCamp**:
> "If your nervous system feels tired, it is not a personal failing. It is a reasonable response to the moment we are living in."

### When to use
- "The tension" or "the honest truth" section
- Before the main sell
- In body copy between dramatic images

## The "who this isn't for" pattern

Explicit disqualification. Builds trust. Filters the wrong fit before they pay.

### Template
Two-column, or bulleted list:
> **This is for you if...**
> - [Honest, specific description]
> - [...]
>
> **This might not be the right fit if...**
> - [Honest, specific counter-description]
> - [...]

### Examples

**Facilitator Training**:
> "This might not be the right fit if you are looking for a weekend or fast-track certification."
> "This might not be the right fit if you are not willing to engage with your own inner work first."

### When to use
- Residential training pages
- Certification pages
- Anywhere the price or commitment is significant

### When NOT to use
- Free workshops (they're already low-commitment, no need to filter)
- Inner Journey self-paced (it's already low-barrier)

## The "space between" pattern

Short sentences. Paragraph breaks. Visual breathing room. The copy itself performs the thing the page is about.

### Template
Break what could be one paragraph into three. Use short sentences as emphasis.

### Example

**Don't write this**:
> "BreathCamp is a 7-day residential retreat at ASHA Retreat Centre in Tuscany, where you will experience a combination of breathwork, movement, sound healing, and community immersion, designed to help you deepen your personal practice."

**Write this**:
> "BreathCamp is a 7-day residential retreat.
>
> ASHA, in Tuscany. Breath, movement, sound, and the kind of community that only happens when you live and practice alongside others for a full week.
>
> Come as you are. Take what lands. Leave the rest."

## The "lived experience" pattern

Speak from practice, not from a pedestal. The founder voice.

### Template
First-person from Anthony or the facilitator, grounded in specific moments.

### Examples

**Regulation Method**:
> "I was once sitting with a high-level client who was mid-panic attack, withdrawing from medication. No technique. No protocol. Just breathing together, with simple counting. That was enough. It started with one breath."

**Facilitator Training**:
> "I built this training because I saw what breathwork did for people who had tried everything else. And I saw that the world needed more facilitators who could hold that space with real skill and real presence."

### When to use
- Founder section
- Hero subhead on flagship pages
- Pull quotes on parallax breaks

## The "factual ritual" pattern

When you need to list outcomes, resist the temptation to dress them up. Say what happens, plainly.

### Template
> "By the end of the week, you will have [specific, concrete thing]. Not [vague outcome]."

### Example

**Regulation Method**:
> "By the end of the week, you leave with tools you trust and the felt confidence to use them."

### When to use
- Outcomes section
- "What you leave with" sections
- Right before the pricing

## The "disclaimer as invitation" pattern

Even disclaimers can sound human. The footer disclaimer is canonical.

### Template
> "[Program name] is an educational program. It is not a substitute for medical or psychological treatment. If you have a mental health condition, please consult your doctor or therapist before beginning."

Compared to typical legal language:
> "Disclaimer: This program is for entertainment purposes only and does not constitute medical advice..."

Our version is shorter, kinder, and clearer.

## How to apply these in a fresh page

When you are writing a new landing page:

1. Write the hero. Use "lived experience" or "we will not promise" in the subhead.
2. Write the "what this is" section. Use "it is not X" here.
3. Write the "who this is for" section. Use "who this isn't for" on the same visual plane.
4. Write the body sections with the "space between" pattern.
5. Write the outcomes section with the "factual ritual" pattern.
6. Write the FAQ closer with a tertiary CTA invitation.
7. Write the footer disclaimer with the "disclaimer as invitation" pattern.

If you have written an entire page and none of these patterns appear, you have written a page that does not sound like AoB. Rewrite.
